Identities, Values & Strengths

9 September 2019

Explain a situation where you have had an ethical decision to make. Discuss how you weighed up the values involved in that decision, the decision you made and the consequences of the action you took.

A decision I have made as a dance business owner is the decision to allow dancers to swap traditional gendered roles in class. West Coast Swing is a partner dance style, where there is a (typically male) leader and a (typically female) follower. Other dance styles/studios have expressly forbidden males from following in class, because some male leaders have expressed dislike of having to dance with someone who is the same gender (there seems to be much less concern about female leaders). Their fear is of putting new students off the dance style (and subsequent financial implications from these individuals not attending dance classes). This goes against what our dance school values - we want to create a fun and inclusive environment in which our students feel safe to create and express themselves. We have normalised role-swapping in our classes, and have changed our vocabulary from "guys and girls" to "leaders and followers". We would rather have our community be accepting and inclusive than to change our values for a few newcomers who might feel uncomfortable dancing with someone of the same gender.

Describe how your culture has influenced your values and identity.

My parents are both immigrants to New Zealand, making me a first-generation New Zealander. My father is Chinese and grew up on a plantation in Malaysia as one of 14 children. My mother is mixed Sri Lankan/Dutch, and was raised in Australia. They learned that to progress in life, you have to be determined and to work hard, and this is a work ethic they have passed down to me. Being part of a small family unit (everyone outside of my immediate family lives overseas) has imparted the value of family and connection. Coming from such a mixed ethnic background, culture for me becomes a bit more confusing. Despite my heritage, I don't have much of a connection to my ethnic roots (apart from a love of Asian food!!). I identify as a New Zealander.

Evaluate your strengths and limitations in terms of your learning and career development. Identify which of your strengths might help you in your learning journey and how they might intersect with learning obstacles.

I am a driven and fast learner who loves solving problems, which will help me along my learning journey as I pick up new concepts and technologies. Empathy is a core skill of mine - I love connecting with those around me, and often find myself in roles of counselling and mentorship. I take a lot of joy in helping to build up those around me. I am enthusiastic and outgoing, which I believe brings a great energy to a team - something which is quite important through such an intense course and in the worforce. I am also highly adaptable and used to a constantly changing environment, which I believe will do me well in the ever-changing world of technology.

I do find that I have a strong need to prove myself and to excel in whatever I do, across all aspects of my life. While this drives me to succeed, it can also lead to significant stress and burnout, and to fluctuations in self-esteem when things don't pan out quite as intended. Having experienced burnout in my previous career, I am aware that this is something I am susceptible to - and I am setting goals around prioritising self-care because of this. I will also need to careful around balancing my time on course with my other commitments - in particular with running my dance business (I will need to have Tuesday evenings away from study for this).

Share an example from your experience of where you were trying to work productively with others, but there was resistance or tension. Discuss the strategies you used at that time, how effective they were, and your reflections on what other strategies you would try now, and why.

One example that springs to mind comes from my time working in medicine. I was working with a particular consultant (senior doctor) who had a long reputation of bullying behaviour. I regularly had to conduct clinics with them, where I would review a patient and then report back to them to discuss the diagnosis and form a treatment plan. I learned that asking questions was a quick pathway to a negative experience. I knew that trying to get them to change their behaviour was futile (the many preceding years of complaints had yet to make a substantive change) - so I instead modified my own behaviour. They seemed to find direct questions threatening, as if I was questioning their clinical judgement. So I instead would ask gentler questions, stating that I "just wanted to make sure that I had understood correctly". Flipping questions around in this way led to much more comfortable work experiences. Given the power imbalance that existed in this work relationship, I feel that this was a helpful and appropriate strategy - and indeed I have given this advice to other doctors/students that went on to work with this consultant after me.
